\begin{abstract}
This report studies semantic segmentation on Pascal VOC 2007 under a shared optimization and evaluation pipeline that compares a classical encoder-decoder baseline, a stronger CNN decoder, two pretrained transformer segmenters, and a lightweight SAM2-based semantic adapter. Because these systems start from different pretrained sources (ImageNet, ADE20K, and SAM2 pretraining), the study should be read as a pragmatic system-level benchmark rather than as a clean architecture-isolation experiment. The official validation split of \OfficialValCount{} images is treated as a locked test set, while the official training split is partitioned into \InternalTrainCount{} internal training images and \InternalDevCount{} internal development images for model selection. Across \HeadlineModelCount{} representative systems, \TopModelName{} achieves the best official-validation overlap metrics at \TopModelMiou{}\% mIoU, while \BestHdModelName{} attains the best HD95 at \BestHdValue{}. The ablations show that model capacity, augmentation strength, and adapter tuning strategy matter more than swapping among simple loss formulations, and that the old 60-epoch budget would have materially under-trained the strongest U-Net backbone. The resulting system goes beyond the assignment minimum by evaluating \CompletedTrainRunCount{} trained runs, reporting both per-class IoU and per-class accuracy, analyzing subset-specific and class-confusion failure modes, and adding both bootstrap and alternate-split robustness checks under a publication-style protocol.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Semantic segmentation remains a useful stress test for dense prediction because good performance requires both recognition and localization. Pascal VOC 2007 is small enough to make architectural and optimization choices visible, yet varied enough to expose the weaknesses of under-powered decoders, insufficient augmentation, and brittle boundary handling \citep{everingham2010pascal}. That combination makes it a good benchmark for a course project that aims to compare families of models rather than merely reproduce one paper.

The project requirements ask for U-Net and SAM, at least one additional model family, quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and at least two ablations. I extend that baseline in three ways. First, I evaluate \HeadlineModelCount{} representative headline systems spanning CNN, encoder-decoder transformer, mask-query transformer, and foundation-model design patterns. Second, I run a larger ablation suite over backbone capacity, loss, augmentation, and SAM2 tuning, including a LoRA-rank sensitivity study for SAM2. Third, I use a stricter reporting pipeline with a locked official validation split, bootstrap and alternate-split robustness checks, explicit per-class accuracy reporting, and both subset-level and class-confusion failure diagnostics. The goal is not to claim a new state of the art, but to produce a careful small-scale study that explains what actually works under a realistic compute budget. Because the pretrained starting points differ across families, the paper focuses on a practical comparison of deployable systems rather than on a perfectly matched architecture-only ablation.

\section{Dataset and Experimental Protocol}

Pascal VOC 2007 provides pixel-wise semantic labels for 20 foreground classes plus background \citep{everingham2010pascal}. I preserve the official validation split as a final test set and never tune on it. The official training split contains 209 labeled images, which I partition into \InternalTrainCount{} internal training images and \InternalDevCount{} internal development images using a deterministic label-aware split heuristic. This internal development partition is used for early stopping, learning-rate control, and checkpoint selection; all headline tables in the report are reported on the untouched official validation split of \OfficialValCount{} images. Because the internal development set is very small and does not represent every class equally, I treat it as a noisy model-selection signal rather than as a second benchmark. In particular, the realized split omits the bicycle class entirely and contains only one or two examples for several other rare classes. To quantify how sensitive the strongest systems are to this choice, I also rerun DeepLabV3+ and SegFormer-B2 on two alternate label-aware training/development partitions built with split seeds 7 and 19, while keeping the official validation set locked throughout.

All models operate on ImageNet-normalized RGB inputs and use a \num{512} pixel resolution during evaluation. Training-time preprocessing depends on the augmentation preset but always maintains paired image-mask geometry, respects the VOC ignore label, and ends with tensor conversion plus normalization. The default \emph{standard} augmentation preset uses random scale jitter, random crop-or-pad to \num{512}, horizontal flips, moderate color jitter, and light Gaussian blur. The ablation suite swaps this preset with \emph{none} and \emph{strong} variants to test whether a small benchmark still benefits from more aggressive appearance perturbations.

In addition to the raw split, I annotate the validation images with four subset tags intended to support targeted failure analysis: person-present (\PersonCount{} images), small-object (\SmallObjectCount{} images), crowded-scene (\CrowdedCount{} images), and high-boundary-complexity (\BoundaryCount{} images). These tags are computed from mask geometry rather than hand-written labels, which keeps the subset analysis reproducible and inexpensive, but they should be interpreted as descriptive proxies rather than as canonical semantic regimes.

\section{Models}

The first headline model is a U-Net-style encoder-decoder with a ResNet encoder, bilinear upsampling, and skip-connected decoder blocks \citep{ronneberger2015u,he2016deep}. This family is intentionally plain: it provides a strong and interpretable baseline, and it is also the base architecture for the backbone, loss, and augmentation ablations, so I do not retrofit it with ASPP-style context modules or a more specialized decoder. To keep the headline comparison from depending on an unnecessarily weak instance, the main table uses the strongest completed plain U-Net variant, with a ResNet-50 encoder pretrained on ImageNet. The ResNet-34 configuration remains the default reference point inside the loss and augmentation ablations because those studies were designed around that cheaper base model.

The second model is DeepLabV3+ with a ResNet-50 backbone and atrous spatial pyramid pooling \citep{chen2018encoder}. DeepLabV3+ is included because it remains a strong CNN segmentation baseline and provides a useful reference point for whether a transformer is buying real performance or merely additional complexity.

The third model is SegFormer-B2, loaded from the pretrained ADE20K checkpoint exposed by the Hugging Face implementation \citep{xie2021segformer}. SegFormer is attractive here because it combines strong multiscale reasoning with a relatively light decoder, making it a good candidate for a small but diverse dataset like VOC.

The fourth headline model is Mask2Former with a Swin-Tiny backbone, again initialized from a pretrained ADE20K semantic-segmentation checkpoint \citep{cheng2022masked}. Unlike SegFormer, Mask2Former predicts a set of class-conditional mask queries and then aggregates them into a dense semantic map. To keep optimization comparable across the benchmark, I collapse the query outputs into semantic logits and optimize those logits with the same dense supervision used for the other models rather than switching the whole training stack over to Mask2Former's native bipartite matching loss. This preserves a common evaluation and loss interface while still testing whether a query-based decoder adds value on a small semantic benchmark.

The final headline family is a semantic segmentation adapter built on top of the SAM2 Hiera-S image encoder \citep{ravi2024sam2}. Rather than using prompt-based mask decoding, the project repurposes SAM2 features for dense semantic prediction via a shallow convolutional decoder. Two variants are studied: a frozen-encoder baseline and a LoRA-enabled version that applies low-rank adapters to attention and MLP submodules inside the image encoder \citep{hu2022lora}. This isolates whether foundation-model features are already strong enough for dense semantic transfer, or whether lightweight task adaptation is essential.

The pretrained starting points differ materially across families: U-Net and DeepLabV3+ use ImageNet ResNet weights, SegFormer and Mask2Former use ADE20K semantic-segmentation checkpoints, and SAM2 starts from the published SAM2 image encoder. This mismatch is acceptable for a practical benchmark, but it means the headline comparison conflates architecture choice with the strength of the transfer prior.

\section{Training Setup}

All experiments share the same optimization policy. Training uses AdamW with model-specific learning rates, a plateau scheduler with a maximum budget of 150 epochs, learning-rate reduction patience of five validation epochs, and early stopping patience of 15 validation epochs after a minimum of 20 epochs \citep{loshchilov2019decoupled}. Validation mIoU is the sole monitored quantity for checkpointing, learning-rate scheduling, and stopping decisions. This policy deliberately treats the epoch limit as a safety ceiling rather than as the definition of convergence. Consistent with that setup, I use official-validation mIoU as the primary ranking metric in the headline comparison, while Dice, pixel accuracy, and HD95 are treated as secondary diagnostics.

The default loss is a weighted sum of cross-entropy and Dice, with two U-Net ablations that replace it by pure cross-entropy or pure Dice. The same dense objective is also applied to the aggregated semantic logits produced by Mask2Former so that all headline models are compared under a common optimization interface. Batch sizes depend on memory footprint: U-Net runs at batch size 8 except for the ResNet-50 variant, DeepLabV3+ uses batch size 4, and the transformer- and SAM2-based models use batch size 1 with gradient accumulation where needed. Mixed precision is disabled on MPS because the relevant kernels were less reliable than full precision in this environment. All evaluation metrics are computed on a common \num{512}$\times$\num{512} resize-and-pad canvas with padded pixels ignored. HD95 is averaged over foreground class-image pairs in which the class is present, which makes it a useful boundary-sensitive diagnostic but a noisier aggregate than mIoU.

All training was performed on a MacBook Pro with an Apple M5 Max and 64 GB unified memory. That hardware is not a conventional segmentation workstation, but it is representative of the kind of single-device environment in which a course project is actually executed. Reporting training hours and evaluation throughput is therefore not incidental; it is part of the practical comparison.

\section{Ablation Studies}

Table~\ref{tab:ablations} summarizes the ablation suite. The backbone study is the clearest result: scaling the U-Net encoder from ResNet-34 to ResNet-50 provides a large accuracy gain, while shrinking to ResNet-18 collapses performance. That asymmetry suggests the baseline U-Net was primarily capacity-limited rather than over-regularized. The loss ablation is subtler. Pure Dice and pure cross-entropy both underperform the default mixed objective, which is consistent with the idea that cross-entropy stabilizes class competition while Dice improves overlap on sparse foreground regions. Augmentation also matters, but not in a monotone way: removing augmentation hurts materially, while the stronger preset recovers most of that loss without surpassing the default standard policy. Within this table, ResNet-34 remains the reference row because the loss and augmentation variants were all executed on that cheaper backbone, even though ResNet-50 is the strongest plain U-Net instance used in the headline comparison.

The SAM2 tuning ablation is especially informative. The frozen adapter is a useful proof of concept, but LoRA improves it by \SamLoRAGain{} mIoU points, which is one of the largest gains in the entire study. In other words, SAM2 features are not enough on their own; lightweight task adaptation is the difference between an interesting transfer baseline and a genuinely competitive model. For that reason, SAM2-LoRA rather than SAM2-Frozen is used as the representative SAM2 system in the main comparison.

Because that result depends on a low-rank adapter, Table~\ref{tab:lora-rank} varies the LoRA rank while keeping the rest of the SAM2 recipe fixed. The goal is not to optimize SAM2 exhaustively, but to test whether the gain is hypersensitive to one arbitrary adapter setting. Under the present budget, the qualitative pattern is diminishing returns: low-rank adaptation already captures most of the transfer benefit, while pushing the rank higher does not automatically buy a better semantic segmenter.

The result is stronger than that generic expectation. The smallest tested adapter, rank 4, actually improves official-validation performance to \num{46.3}\% mIoU, beating the original rank-8 setup by \num{2.1} points. Increasing the rank to 16 moves in the opposite direction, dropping to \num{37.7}\% mIoU. Under this dataset and budget, the SAM2 adapter is therefore not rank-starved; if anything, the larger update appears harder to optimize cleanly.

\section{Generalization and Failure Analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:subset-heatmap} reports mIoU over the four tagged validation subsets in addition to the aggregate score. Because these subset tags are geometry-derived proxies rather than curated semantic regimes, I use them for descriptive failure analysis rather than for strong causal claims. The crowded-scene subset is nevertheless the hardest regime for every model in the study, which is unsurprising because crowded masks combine class ambiguity, occlusion, and more boundary transitions. Small-object and high-boundary-complexity cases are also substantially harder than the overall average. The encouraging result is that the overall mIoU ranking remains fairly stable across subsets: the best global models are also the most reliable specialized models. The disappointing result is that no model closes the gap on crowded scenes, so this remains the clearest open problem in the project.

Figure~\ref{fig:qualitative-headline} makes the ranking more concrete. The easy row shows that all models can solve a clean single-object scene once the foreground occupies enough pixels. The crowded and articulated-person rows are more revealing. U-Net tends to fragment masks or miss object extent entirely, DeepLabV3+ usually recovers the right coarse layout but can blur fine boundaries, and the stronger pretrained transformers tend to maintain the best compromise between global context and local structure. SAM2-LoRA is substantially stronger than the frozen adapter, but it still tends to miss fine extremities or exact object extent under clutter.

That matters because it shows that the remaining failures are mostly about fine-grained category boundaries and object extent, not complete failure of scene understanding.

\section{Lessons Learned and Limitations}

Several lessons stand out. First, pretrained transformer features are extremely effective even on a relatively small benchmark, provided the decoder is designed for dense prediction; the added Mask2Former run shows that decoder/query design still matters materially even when the transfer prior is similarly strong. Second, classical CNN systems remain competitive: DeepLabV3+ stays relevant as a strong boundary model even when the transformer family is stronger on the primary overlap metric, so the preferred model depends on whether region overlap or boundary distance is the primary objective. Third, the right training policy matters almost as much as the architecture itself. The backbone ablation would have been misread under the old fixed 60-epoch cap, and the LoRA result would have been easy to miss if SAM2 were evaluated only as a frozen feature extractor.

The project also has clear limitations. VOC 2007 is small, so architecture ranking may be more sensitive to the inductive biases of the benchmark than to true large-scale robustness. More importantly, the pretrained initializations differ across families, so the headline comparison should be read as a pragmatic benchmark of end-to-end systems rather than as a clean architecture-only study. The official validation split is treated correctly as a test set, but the internal development split is still only 31 images, missing bicycle entirely and representing several classes only once. The added alternate-split reruns help show that the ranking of the established non-SAM baselines is not a complete artifact of one specific seed, but they still cover only two architectures and three split realizations rather than a full cross-validated study, and they were not extended to Mask2Former. The hardware environment also shapes what is practical: larger transformer variants or full SAM2 fine-tuning were deliberately excluded because they would not have been a responsible use of time on the available device. Finally, the report still evaluates boundary quality only on the common \num{512}$\times$\num{512} canvas rather than at original image resolution.

\section{Conclusion}

This study benchmarks \HeadlineModelCount{} representative segmentation systems and an expanded set of ablations and robustness checks on Pascal VOC 2007 under a common, validation-driven training pipeline. \TopModelName{} is the strongest model by the primary overlap metric, \BestHdModelName{} is the best HD95 model, and SAM2 becomes competitive only after lightweight LoRA adaptation. The ablation suite shows that encoder capacity, augmentation strength, and training budget matter materially, while the default cross-entropy-plus-Dice loss remains a sound compromise. The added robustness analyses sharpen that picture: SegFormer-B2 stays stable across alternate internal splits, DeepLabV3+ is much more split-sensitive, and the SAM2 rank sweep shows that more LoRA capacity is not automatically better. More broadly, the project demonstrates that even a course-scale benchmark can support a meaningful empirical paper if the evaluation protocol is locked, the figures are interpreted carefully, and the conclusions are stated at the level the evidence actually supports.
